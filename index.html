The Platform

Eight Pillars of Reform

These aren't talking points. They're actionable policy reforms designed to dismantle the systems that allow corporations and special interests to hold more power than the citizens they profit from.

Pillar 01 of 08
End Corporate Personhood in Politics
Overturn Citizens United. Corporations are not people.
The Problem
In 2010, the Supreme Court ruled in Citizens United v. FEC that corporations have the same First Amendment rights as individual citizens, opening the floodgates for unlimited corporate money in politics. Since then, billions of dollars have poured into Super PACs, effectively allowing corporations to buy influence over the representatives who are supposed to serve the people. Your vote competes with a Fortune 500 lobbying budget. That's not democracy.
The Reform
Pass a constitutional amendment to overturn Citizens United and end the legal classification of corporations as citizens for the purpose of political donations. Corporations can exist, profit, and thrive — but they cannot buy representation. Political donations will be limited to individual citizens with hard caps, and Super PACs funded by corporate money will be dissolved. Representatives will answer to voters, not shareholders.
Current Reality
$14.4 billion spent on federal elections in 2020. The majority from corporate-funded PACs and dark money groups.
After Reform
Elections funded by individual citizens only. Hard caps on donations. Full transparency. No dark money.
Pillar 02 of 08
Single-Issue Bills Only
One bill, one issue. No more poison pills and pork barrels.
The Problem
Congress routinely bundles unrelated legislation into massive omnibus bills — thousands of pages long — that no one fully reads before voting. This allows lawmakers to slip in special-interest provisions, wasteful spending, and controversial policies under the cover of "must-pass" legislation. Veterans' benefits get tied to corporate tax loopholes. Disaster relief gets held hostage for political earmarks. The American people never get a clean vote on the issues that matter.
The Reform
Require all federal legislation to address a single issue. Every bill gets a clear title, a defined scope, and a standalone vote. No riders. No bundling. No 4,000-page omnibus packages passed at 2 AM. If a policy can't survive on its own merits, it doesn't deserve to become law. Representatives will be forced to take clear, public positions on individual issues — and voters will finally be able to hold them accountable.
Current Reality
The 2024 spending bill was 1,012 pages. Fewer than 10% of Congress read it in full before voting.
After Reform
Every bill is readable, debatable, and addresses exactly one issue. Transparent governance by default.
Pillar 03 of 08
Usury Law Reform — Cap Interest at 5%
Stop predatory lending. Protect working Americans from financial exploitation.
The Problem
The average credit card interest rate in America is over 24%. Payday lenders charge rates that can exceed 400% APR. These aren't financial services — they're traps designed to keep working families in permanent debt. Existing usury laws are full of loopholes, and federal preemption allows national banks to export high rates from states with no caps at all. The financial industry profits billions annually from Americans who can least afford it.
The Reform
Update federal usury laws to establish a hard cap of 5% maximum interest on all consumer lending — credit cards, personal loans, auto loans, and payday lending. Close the loopholes that allow banks to dodge state-level caps. Lending should be a service, not a predatory industry. A 5% cap still allows lenders to profit while ensuring that borrowing money doesn't become a life sentence of debt.
Current Average
Credit cards: 24.7% APR. Payday loans: 400%+ APR. Auto subprime: 14%+ APR.
After Reform
Maximum 5% APR across all consumer lending. No exceptions. No loopholes. No predatory lending.
Pillar 04 of 08
The American Business Score
A corporate credit score that rewards companies for investing in America.
The Problem
American corporations receive enormous tax breaks and subsidies regardless of whether they actually benefit the American people. Companies lay off thousands of workers while posting record profits and issuing massive shareholder dividends. They outsource jobs, exploit visa programs to undercut American wages, and sell products that actively harm public health — all while paying lower effective tax rates than a schoolteacher. There's no accountability between what a business takes from America and what it gives back.
The Reform
Establish the American Business Score (ABS) — a transparent, publicly visible rating system for every business operating in the United States. The score determines the level of tax incentives, government contracts, and regulatory benefits a company receives. Businesses that invest in Americans earn advantages. Businesses that exploit them don't.
  • ABS-1 American Workforce Ratio — What percentage of your workforce is American? Companies that hire domestically score higher. Mass layoffs while reporting profit growth tank your score.
  • ABS-2 Layoff-to-Profit Ratio — If you're cutting jobs while shareholder profits are rising, your score drops. You can't claim hardship while issuing dividends.
  • ABS-3 Product Impact Score — Is your product good for Americans? Measured across health outcomes, economic impact, and consumer safety. Companies selling harmful products at scale take a hit.
  • ABS-4 H-1B Dependency — Companies that rely heavily on H-1B visas to fill roles that qualified Americans could do see a reduced score. The visa program should supplement the workforce, not replace it.
  • ABS-5 Tax Break Accountability — Your ABS directly determines your eligibility for tax incentives. High score = full benefits. Low score = standard rates. No more free rides.
Current Reality
Companies receive tax breaks regardless of behavior. No correlation between public benefit and public subsidy.
After Reform
Tax incentives tied directly to measurable contributions to the American workforce and public good.
Pillar 05 of 08
Transparent Pricing Act
What you see is what you pay. No hidden fees. No mystery bills.
The Problem
A hospital can charge you $47 for a single aspirin and $800 for a blood test that costs $12 to run. Your phone bill has "regulatory fees" that the company invented. Your concert ticket costs $45 until checkout adds $63 in "service fees." The American consumer has no idea what anything actually costs until it's too late. This isn't a free market — it's a rigged one.
The Reform
Require all-in pricing across healthcare, financial services, telecommunications, and ticketing. Hospitals and pharmaceutical companies must publish actual procedure and drug costs. Banks must disclose the true total cost of any financial product upfront. No drip pricing, no hidden surcharges, no "fees" that appear only at checkout. The price you see is the price you pay. Violations carry meaningful fines — not the cost-of-doing-business slaps that companies currently budget for.
Current Reality
The same hospital procedure can be billed at $1,500 or $15,000 depending on your insurance. Consumers have no way to comparison shop.
After Reform
Mandatory published pricing. All-in costs at point of sale. Consumers can compare and choose. The market actually works.
Pillar 06 of 08
Ban Congressional Stock Trading
If you regulate an industry, you don't get to profit from its stock.
The Problem
Members of Congress sit on committees that regulate industries — and then trade stocks in those same industries. They receive classified briefings, shape legislation, and vote on bills that directly impact share prices — all while their personal portfolios benefit. Studies consistently show that Congressional stock trades outperform the market at rates that would trigger an SEC investigation for anyone else. The STOCK Act of 2012 was supposed to fix this. It didn't. Penalties are as low as $200, and enforcement is nearly nonexistent.
The Reform
Outright ban on individual stock trading for all sitting members of Congress and their immediate family members. No exceptions. No blind trusts with convenient timing. All personal investments must be moved into broad-based index funds or U.S. Treasury bonds within 90 days of taking office. This applies to spouses, dependent children, and any entity controlled by the member. You want to serve the public? Then your portfolio serves the public too. When the whole market does well, you do well. That's the only acceptable alignment of incentives.
Current Reality
Members of Congress traded over $630 million in stocks in 2023. Many trades coincided suspiciously with committee actions and policy announcements.
After Reform
Index funds and Treasury bonds only — for members and immediate family. Your financial interests are the country's financial interests. Period.
  • SCOPE Members of Congress — All sitting Representatives and Senators. No exceptions for seniority, committee assignment, or party.
  • FAMILY Immediate family — Spouses, dependent children, and any LLCs, trusts, or entities controlled by the member or family members.
  • ALLOWED Broad index funds & Treasuries — S&P 500, total market index, U.S. Treasury bonds. You win when America wins.
  • TIMELINE 90-day divestiture — All non-compliant holdings must be liquidated or transferred within 90 days of swearing in.
Pillar 07 of 08
Anti-Corporate Housing Act
Houses are for families. Not for hedge funds to hoard.
The Problem
Institutional investors — BlackRock, Invitation Homes, private equity firms — have purchased hundreds of thousands of single-family homes across America, converting them into rental properties and driving up prices for everyone else. In some markets, corporate buyers account for over 25% of home purchases. They pay cash, outbid families, and then charge rent that's higher than a mortgage would be. An entire generation is being locked out of homeownership not because they can't afford a house, but because they're competing against firms managing trillions in assets.
The Reform
Ban institutional investors from purchasing single-family homes. Corporations and investment funds with over $50 million in assets under management are prohibited from acquiring single-family residential properties. Existing corporate-held single-family inventory must be divested over a 5-year period, with right-of-first-refusal given to current tenants, followed by first-time homebuyers. Housing is infrastructure for families and communities — it is not an asset class for Wall Street to extract wealth from.
Current Reality
Corporate investors own 574,000+ single-family rentals. In cities like Atlanta, they buy 1 in 4 homes sold. Median home prices have outpaced wages for 20 consecutive years.
After Reform
Corporations out of the single-family market. Existing inventory divested to tenants and first-time buyers. Homes go to people, not portfolios.
Pillar 08 of 08
Right to Repair
You bought it. You own it. You can fix it.
The Problem
When you buy a tractor, a phone, a wheelchair, or a car — you should own it. But manufacturers increasingly use software locks, proprietary parts, and restrictive licensing to prevent you from repairing the things you paid for. John Deere sells farmers $500,000 tractors that can only be serviced by authorized dealers. Apple has fought for years to prevent independent repair shops from fixing iPhones. Medical device companies charge hospitals thousands for repairs that cost pennies in parts. This isn't about intellectual property — it's about forcing consumers into a cycle of dependency and planned obsolescence.
The Reform
Pass a comprehensive federal Right to Repair Act. Manufacturers must make diagnostic tools, replacement parts, and repair documentation available to consumers and independent repair shops at fair market prices. Software locks that prevent repair are prohibited. Warranty voiding for third-party repairs is banned. If a company sells you a product, you have the unrestricted right to maintain, repair, and modify it. This applies to consumer electronics, agricultural equipment, medical devices, automobiles, and industrial machinery.
Current Reality
Manufacturers use software locks and parts restrictions to force authorized-only repairs. A $15 fix becomes a $500 dealer visit. Farmers can't repair their own tractors during harvest.
After Reform
Full access to parts, tools, and documentation. Independent repair shops can compete. You own what you buy — completely.

Transparency

Why anonymous?

Not because there's something to hide. Because there's something to prove.

Ideas over identity

Every political campaign in modern history lives or dies on the candidate's personality, media presence, and fundraising ability. This one lives or dies on eight policy proposals. If the ideas are bad, no amount of charisma should save them.

No corporate attack surface

The moment a candidate is named, the opposition research machine fires up, funded by the very corporations these reforms threaten. By staying anonymous during the platform phase, the ideas get oxygen before the smear campaigns start.

Accountability through policy

When the candidate is revealed, they will be bound to this platform publicly. No pivoting. No "evolving positions." These eight pillars are the contract. Published first, committed to permanently.

A new kind of campaign

What if a political campaign started with "here's what I'll do" instead of "here's who I am"? This is an experiment in substance-first politics. If it fails, at least we tried something different.
